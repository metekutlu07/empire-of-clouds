Mete Kutlu

Mete Kutlu is a creative technologist, new media artist, and researcher based in Paris. Trained as an architect at ENSA Paris-Malaquais, he graduated in 2016 and subsequently worked across Japan, China, and South Korea with architects including Kazuyo Sejima, Kengo Kuma, and Wang Shu. Since 2019, he has been teaching Cities in Mirror, an experimental and artistic approach to mapping urban space, at ENSA Paris-Belleville, where he is also an associate researcher at the IPRAUS laboratory.

His work operates at the intersection of premodern metaphysics and contemporary computation. Focusing on the medieval Silk Road, he identifies an algorithmic tradition of art and a computational cosmology that resonates with today's cloud-based infrastructures. Through a series of research residencies across this historical corridor, he develops a method grounded in the confrontation between historical artistic traditions and contemporary digital media, including photogrammetry, augmented reality, and machine learning.

He was awarded the Daniel Arasse Prize by the Académie française and the École française de Rome. He has conducted research at the Villa Medici in Rome, the Monastery of San Giorgio Maggiore in Venice, and the French Palace in Istanbul. His investigations into patterned and cosmological visual systems have led to archival work at the Vatican Apostolic Archive, the Topkapı Palace, and the Biblioteca Nazionale Marciana. In parallel, he develops large-scale digital projects that translate historical cosmologies into immersive computational environments. His work includes augmented reality applications, volumetric reconstructions, and generative simulations presented in Carthage, Paris, and Seoul. In 2022, he received the Innovative Digital Services grant for Spolia Botanique, an immersive botanical project developed in partnership with the Château de Chaumont-sur-Loire.

His work unfolds between ancient myth and science-fictional narratives. It advances a non-anthropocentric vision of culture, a cosmic perspective on technology, and a non-Eurocentric history of civilization. The world is understood as a computational system of symbols, a cosmopolitan and inclusive ecology of intelligence. His experimental methods of design and research are grounded in transversal, non-linear, and divergent thinking. Drawing on hacker culture, he adopts a mode of inquiry based on the subversion of established conventions and institutional frameworks. Rather than following preplanned structures, he develops bottom-up processes in which randomness and accident become central forces of creativity. Forms and ideas emerge through iterative and generative processes. Against the sobriety of modernist abstraction, his work proposes a poetic vision of civilization in which metaphor and analogy replace rational description. Vibrant colors and patterns become the medium of these alternative narratives, revealing imaginary worlds embedded within both historical and technological systems.

Paris, 2026.
