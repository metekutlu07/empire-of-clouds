The Scripted Universe

There was a time when language measured the universe. Long before machine code, we imagined a divine script of the cosmos.

Along the medieval Silk Road, the universe was conceived as a projected image generated from a celestial book of codes. In this alternative genealogy, the history of computation begins with creation itself. Before the world, there was a cloud, the raw material of being. God was imagined as a calligrapher dwelling within a misty scriptorium. With pen and tablet, the cosmic script was inscribed.

These codes shaped the cloud through vibrations. Every being emerged as a generated image, each form the visible trace of an underlying code. In this alternative genealogy, the history of computation begins with creation itself.

From Baghdad to Black Holes

The code lived in mathematics. It was in medieval Baghdad that algorithms were invented. These mathematicians laid the foundations for the geometries that later allowed Einstein to revolutionize our understanding of space. This algorithmic tradition did not remain within manuscripts but became the decor of daily life. Turco-Persian mosaics depicted complex patterns modern scientists believed impossible to exist until their discovery just twenty years ago in extraterrestrial minerals on a meteorite.

Today, string theorists and quantum physicists revive a forgotten metaphor of the Silk Road: the pen and paper. Learning from black holes, the cosmos begins to appear fundamentally flat, like a sheet of paper. Between the manuscript and the quantum computer, we find a cosmopolitan history of a universe built of information rather than matter.

The Original Hackers

Before software, there was the talisman. In Herat, Afghanistan, the “Silicon Valley” of the 15th century, scientists produced advanced analog computers known as astrolabes. Scholars practiced ilm-i huruf, the science of letters. Organized in secretive networks, these lettrists sought to manipulate the algorithmic fabric of existence. They were the ancestors of the contemporary hacker, connecting to an intangible repository of knowledge they, too, called "The Cloud."

We have been writing programs on metal reflective disks, once by hand, then by lasers. Long before we came up with digital computers, we used language, light and metals in attempts at creating and controlling virtual, intangible worlds. Once they yielded in imaginary, metaphysical worlds, now they form immersive technological environments.

The Encoded City

Information was the fabric of urban space. Along the Silk Road, buildings were books of stone; cities were written and read. In a secret manuscript of the Ottoman sultans, Constantinople appears as a living cipher, an urban space woven from cryptic algorithms of prophecy. The variegated worlds of miniatures reflected this urban encryption.

Both back then and now, information flows through maze-like pathways, inscribed in quartz crystals. Once, these cristalline circuits carried the words of God, imagined to be made of divine light. Now, inscribed with ultraviolet light, they carry data and the commands of artificial intelligence, coursing as electrons. The juxtaposition is not a mere analogy, but a deep material, functional and visual convergence.

From Sophia to Data

The book concludes with three digital experiments. Through 3D modelling of a miniature, we take a stroll inside an imaginary Constantinople. By blending French rationalism with Ottoman mysticism, the research reveals the shared origins of The Matrix (1999) and Ottoman myths in the Jewish Kabbala. Through pixel interpolation and procedural design, we explore the decisive shift in human creativity. We witness the final transformation of the sacred: the algorithmic evolution of Hagia Sophia into Hagia Data.
